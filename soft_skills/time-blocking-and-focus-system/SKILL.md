# Time Blocking and Focus System

## Purpose
A time blocking and focus system is the discipline of structuring a calendar to ensure that high-impact work receives the necessary time and cognitive capacity. In leadership roles, the calendar is a scorecard of priorities. Without intentional defense of time, leaders become reactive, responding to urgency rather than driving strategic outcomes. This system ensures that "deep work" is prioritized and that energy is managed across the day.

## Core Principles

### 1. Control Your Calendar
Recognize that you have significant authority over your schedule. Protecting time for strategic work is a requirement for effective leadership, not a luxury.

### 2. Schedule Around Energy
Cognitive capacity is not constant. Match your most demanding tasks to your peak energy periods (typically early morning) and use lower-energy periods for routine work or meetings.

### 3. Deep Work Requires Contiguous Time
Strategic thinking, complex writing, and deep analysis cannot be done in fragmented 15-minute pockets. Protect blocks of 90 minutes to 2 hours for uninterrupted focus.

### 4. Commit to the Ritual
A time block is only valuable if you follow a focus ritual: no notifications, phone on silent, and working exclusively on one high-impact task.

### 5. Set the Team Tone
A leader's calendar habits influence the entire organization. By protecting focus time, you empower your team to do the same, fostering a culture of productivity over performative busyness.

## Weekly Calendar Architecture

### 1. The Deep Work Block (Early Morning)
- **Focus**: Strategic thinking, writing decision memos, and complex problem-solving.
- **Ritual**: Zero notifications. No email or Slack until the "frog" is eaten.

### 2. Standups & Tactical Syncs (Mid-Morning)
- **Focus**: Tight, 15-30 minute updates to unblock the team and align on daily priorities.

### 3. Execution & Meeting Batches (Late Morning/Early Afternoon)
- **Focus**: 1:1s, stakeholder syncs, and cross-functional collaborations.
- **Strategy**: Batch similar meetings back-to-back to minimize context-switching.

### 4. The Mid-Day Reset
- **Focus**: Physical movement and mental reset to maintain afternoon energy.

### 5. Decision & Review Block (Late Afternoon)
- **Focus**: Protected time for reviewing documents, making pending calls, and clearing administrative blockers.

### 6. Wrap-up & Planning (End of Day)
- **Focus**: Processing follow-ups and identifying the next day's "frog."

## The Daily Ritual: "Eat the Frog"
Identify the single most important and often most difficult task each morning. Complete it during your first deep work block before engaging with any external communication. Tackling the hardest task when your energy is highest ensures significant daily progress regardless of subsequent interruptions.

## The Weekly Review & Plan
Every Friday, spend 30 minutes reviewing the past week's priorities and planning the next week's top three objectives. Ensure that focus blocks are correctly placed and that the calendar aligns with strategic goals.

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] A 90-minute to 2-hour deep work block is scheduled daily before meetings.
- [ ] The "Eat the Frog" task is identified every morning.
- [ ] Meetings are batched by type (e.g., all 1:1s on the same day).
- [ ] Low-energy periods are used for routine tasks or listening-heavy meetings.
- [ ] Notifications are disabled during focus blocks.
- [ ] Time is allocated for a mid-day reset.
- [ ] A weekly review and planning session is scheduled.
- [ ] Non-essential meetings are declined or delegated.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Blocking Without Protecting**: Moving focus blocks for non-urgent requests.
- **Context-Switching**: Allowing Slack or email to interrupt deep work.
- **Back-to-Back Overload**: Failing to build in 10-15 minute buffers between meetings.
- **Unpredictability**: Constantly shifting the days and times of recurring 1:1s or syncs.
- **Neglecting Energy**: Trying to do hard strategic thinking during an afternoon energy dip.
