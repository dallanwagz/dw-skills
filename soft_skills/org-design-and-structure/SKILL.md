# Org Design and Structure

## Purpose
Organizational design is the deliberate process of structuring a team to enable its strategy and maximize its impact. The right structure clarifies ownership, reduces decision-making friction, and creates visible advancement paths for talent. Conversely, a poorly designed organization creates bottlenecks, silos, and management burnout. This skill focuses on building scalable scaffolding that evolves with the business and its people.

## Core Principles

### 1. Structure Follows Strategy
Organizational design should be driven by the team's objectives (what needs to be accomplished) rather than by the specific individuals currently in place. Avoid creating roles solely to satisfy personal expectations if they don't serve the broader strategic mission.

### 2. Manageable Spans of Control
The quality of management degrades as the number of direct reports increases. For most high-growth environments, a span of 5-8 reports per manager is optimal. Exceeding this limit prevents managers from providing effective coaching and strategic oversight.

### 3. Visible Advancement Paths
A sustainable structure provides clear growth opportunities. If the only path to advancement is for a superior to leave, talented individuals will eventually seek growth elsewhere. Design structures that offer both management and non-management (IC) progression.

### 4. Reduce Friction and Silos
Structure should match the natural flow of work. Group individuals who collaborate frequently to minimize cross-team dependencies and handoff delays.

### 5. Separate Headcount from Capability
Adding reports to a manager's team does not automatically increase their leadership level. Readiness for higher-level management (e.g., M1 to M2) is defined by broader strategic thinking and the ability to develop other leaders, not just the size of their organization.

## Organizational Assessment Framework
Before implementing structure changes, analyze:
- **Strategic Horizon (18-24 months)**: Are you scaling for depth, breadth, or speed?
- **Decision Ownership**: Who currently makes critical tactical and strategic decisions, and where are the bottlenecks?
- **Fracture Lines**: Where do dependencies naturally form, and where is collaboration most intensive?
- **Manager Growth Edges**: What are the specific development goals for existing leaders, and how does the new structure support or hinder them?

## Common Organizational Models
- **Functional**: Organized by skill set (Engineering, Design, Product). Best for deep expertise but can create silos.
- **Product-Based**: Organized by specific product or feature areas. Best for clear ownership and speed but can lead to duplicated efforts.
- **Geography-Based**: Organized by location. Necessary for time zone and local infrastructure management but can create "us vs. them" dynamics.
- **Matrixed**: Dual reporting to functional and project leads. High flexibility but carries significant risk of confusion and authority conflicts.

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Structure is explicitly derived from the current and future team strategy.
- [ ] Manager spans are within the 5-8 report range.
- [ ] Critical decisions have a clearly defined and empowered owner in the new structure.
- [ ] Visible advancement or development paths exist for all key roles.
- [ ] The structure minimizes handoffs between teams working on related projects.
- [ ] All impacted managers have been consulted 1:1 before broad communication.
- [ ] The transition plan includes a clear "why" that focuses on team health and effectiveness.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Designing for Individuals**: Creating a structure to avoid difficult people conversations or to reward tenure over strategy.
- **Vague Career Paths**: Promising growth opportunities that are not supported by the actual organizational hierarchy.
- **Overloading Managers**: Ignoring high spans of control, leading to burnout and poor talent development.
- **Reorganizing in Silence**: Failing to explain the logic behind changes, which breeds anxiety and defensive behavior.
- **Matching Structure to Wishful Thinking**: Designing an organization based on how you *hope* work happens rather than how it *actually* happens.
