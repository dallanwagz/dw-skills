# Risk Registers and Pre-Mortems

## Purpose
Proactive risk management is the discipline of identifying, assessing, and mitigating potential threats to a project before they materialize. A Risk Register is a living document that tracks these threats, while a Pre-Mortem is a strategic exercise where a team imagines a future failure to work backward and identify its causes. Together, they shift a team's posture from reactive firefighting to deliberate preparation.

## Core Principles

### 1. Risks Must Be Named to Be Managed
Unspoken concerns are the most dangerous. Formally naming a risk moves it from a vague anxiety to an actionable planning item.

### 2. Pre-Mortems Leverage Hindsight
Imagining that a project *has already failed* allows teams to identify risks more effectively than simply asking what *might* go wrong. This reframing bypasses optimism bias.

### 3. The Risk Register Is Operational
A risk register is only valuable if it is reviewed regularly (weekly or bi-weekly). It must be a dynamic tool that evolves with the project, not a static compliance document.

### 4. Prioritize by Impact and Likelihood
Focus energy on risks that are both likely to occur and catastrophic in impact. Accept or monitor minor, unlikely risks to avoid resource fragmentation.

### 5. Assign Clear Ownership
Every risk must have a designated "watcher" who is responsible for monitoring signals and pulling the trigger on mitigation plans.

## The Pre-Mortem Exercise (60-90 Minutes)

### 1. The Premise
"Imagine it is six months from now and the project has failed completely. It didn't ship, or it shipped and was a disaster. What happened?"

### 2. Individual Brainstorming
Team members spend 10-15 minutes writing down every possible cause of failure independently to avoid groupthink and ensure diverse perspectives.

### 3. The Share-Out
Go around the room and capture every risk on a shared document without debate or dismissal. The goal is exhaustive identification.

### 4. Clustering & Ranking
Group similar risks into themes and rank them on a 2x2 grid of Impact vs. Likelihood.

### 5. Identification of Signals & Tripwires
For the top 3-5 risks, identify:
- **Signal**: The measurable indicator that the risk is materializing.
- **Tripwire**: The specific threshold that triggers an immediate mitigation action.

## The Risk Register Structure
Maintain a document with the following columns for the top 5-10 risks:
- **ID**: Unique identifier.
- **Risk Name**: Specific description of the threat (e.g., "Engineering capacity diverted to critical bugs").
- **Impact/Likelihood**: High/Medium/Low rankings.
- **Signal**: What we are measuring (e.g., "Hours per week on unplanned firefighting").
- **Tripwire**: The trigger point (e.g., ">10 hours/week for two consecutive weeks").
- **Owner**: The individual responsible for monitoring the signal.
- **Mitigation Plan**: Concrete actions to take when the tripwire is hit.
- **Status**: Current assessment (Green/Yellow/Red).

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Pre-mortem session included diverse team perspectives.
- [ ] Risks are specific and actionable, not vague or outside our control.
- [ ] Every high-impact/high-likelihood risk has a mitigation plan.
- [ ] Signals are measurable and tripwires are specific thresholds.
- [ ] Each risk has a single, accountable owner.
- [ ] Risk register is integrated into the weekly operating rhythm.
- [ ] Static or irrelevant risks are retired as the project progresses.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Lack of Follow-through**: Conducting a pre-mortem but failing to create or maintain the risk register.
- **Triggerless Risks**: Identifying a threat but having no specific signal or tripwire to indicate when it’s happening.
- **Risk Overload**: Trying to track too many minor risks, which dilutes focus on the critical few.
- **Static Documentation**: Failing to update the register as the project environment and priorities shift.
- **No Real Ownership**: Assigning risks to "the team" rather than a specific individual.
