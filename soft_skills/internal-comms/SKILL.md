# Internal Communications and Status Reports

Purpose
Communicate progress, risks, and decisions clearly so leaders stay aligned, anxiety stays low, and work moves faster. Your goal is not to “send an update.” Your goal is to earn trust and enable decisions.

Principles
Clarity beats completeness. Executives will reward crisp thinking.
Brevity drives comprehension. Start with the point, then add detail.
Own the narrative. Don’t make readers hunt for what matters.
Bad news early, with a plan. Never surprise leaders late.
Write for the reader. Calibrate detail to their context and stakes.
Recommendations over reports. “We could do A, B, or C. Based on X, I recommend B because…”

Standard Status Template
Use this for weekly or biweekly updates to managers and key stakeholders.

TL;DR
One or two sentences. State outcome and meaning. Example: “Feature X shipped to 25% with 0 Sev-1s. On track for full rollout next Tuesday; no gating risks.”

Current Status
Green/Yellow/Red and one sentence on why. If Yellow/Red, name the constraint and owner.

What Changed Since Last Update
Three short bullets max. Focus on decisions, milestones, and deltas, not activity.

Risks and Issues
List the top one to three. Each includes impact, likelihood, trigger, and mitigation/ask. Example: “Risk: partner API quota may cap throughput by 20%. Trigger: >5k RPS. Mitigation: throttle + batch by 4 pm Friday. Ask: approval to add $8k/mo capacity.”

Next Milestones
Date, milestone, confidence. Keep to three. Be honest on confidence.

Decisions/Asks
State the decision needed, by when, options, and your recommendation with one-line rationale. Make it easy to say yes.

Metrics Snapshot
Only the few that prove health and trajectory. Include target vs. actual and short interpretation, not raw charts.

Crisis/Incident Update Template
Use this for hot issues where leaders need calm, complete signals fast.

Subject: Incident X – Status Update

What happened
Plain language, time-bounded statement.

Customer impact
Who, how many, severity, duration. If unknown, say what you know and when you’ll know more.

Current state
Containment status and service health.

ETA to next update
Time, not “soon.”

Root cause (working theory)
If unknown, give top hypothesis and the data you’re gathering.

Mitigation and path to green
Steps, owners, and ETAs.

Risks and blocks
What could make this worse and what you’re doing to prevent it.

Asks
Resources, approvals, or decisions needed now.

Executive One-Pager Template
Use for monthly or quarterly readouts or when seeking alignment on direction.

Title and date
Clear, specific.

The point
One paragraph that answers “What do you want me to know, decide, or do?”

Context in one graph or two sentences
Why this matters now. Keep history minimal and relevant.

Results to date
Three bullets that quantify impact. Tie to business outcomes.

Plan and milestones
What happens next, by when, and confidence level.

Risks and mitigations
Top two. Show you’re ahead of them.

Decision/ask
Options, your recommendation, rationale, and the cost of delay.

Writing Rules that Lower Anxiety
Close loops. Include owner and ETA for every open thread.
Eliminate jargon. Prefer simple, exact words over clever ones.
Prefer specifics. “June 9, 3 pm PT” beats “next week.”
Put links last. They support the story, they are not the story.
Never bury the lede. First three lines should let a leader decide whether to keep reading.

Cadence and Channels
Routine work gets a weekly update to your manager and core stakeholders using the status template.
High-visibility or cross-functional work gets a monthly one-pager to the broader stakeholder set.
Incidents get timestamped updates at predictable intervals until resolution and postmortem.
Use email or your company’s doc tool for durable updates. Use chat for fast heads-up, then drop the link to the durable artifact.

Examples

Good TL;DR
“Checkout latency down 18% week over week after cache warm fix. Conversion recovered to 3.1% vs 3.0% target; we expect to hit 3.2% by Friday pending partner rollout.”

Weak TL;DR
“We did a lot of work on performance last week and things are looking better.”

Good risk statement
“Carrier certification may slip two weeks due to holiday staffing, which would push launch to July 2 and reduce Q2 revenue by $420k. We can avoid this with an expedited test window costing $12k. Recommend approving.”

Weak risk statement
“Carrier may slip. Working with them.”

Promotion Signal
Leaders who communicate like owners earn trust faster. Translate activity into outcomes, surface risks with plans, frame options with a clear recommendation, and maintain a predictable cadence. Your communication becomes your reputation.
