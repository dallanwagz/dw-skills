# Hiring Scorecards and Loop Design

Purpose
Hire predictably great people by defining what “great” looks like up front, assessing it consistently, and running an interview loop that produces clear, comparable signals with minimal bias and churn.

Voice goal
Be specific and behavioral. Define outcomes, test for them, and decide quickly with evidence.

Principles
Start with outcomes. Scorecards anchor on what the hire must deliver in 6–12 months.
Assess behaviors, not vibes. Use structured questions tied to competencies.
One role, one scorecard. No freelancing competencies mid-loop.
One competency, one owner. Avoid duplicate signals and gaps.
Evidence over opinions. Write what you heard, then the judgment.
Bar raising is deliberate. Compare to the best in-seat performers, not the average.

Role scorecard template
Role outcomes (6–12 months)
List 3–5 measurable outcomes the person must deliver. Example: “Ship v1 of Partner API to GA with ≤0.1% error rate by Q3; achieve 99.95% uptime; onboard two strategic partners by Q4.”

Must-have competencies
Choose 5–7 that predict those outcomes. Examples: Problem Solving/Systems Thinking, Ownership, Customer Obsession, Deliver Results, Cross-Functional Influence, Hiring/Developing Talent, Technical Depth in X.

Nice-to-have competencies
1–3 add-ons that are not gatekeepers. Keep separate in decisions.

Deal-breakers and non-goals
Clarify what this role is not. Example: “Not a research role; we need shipping cadence, not paper writing.”

Leveling expectations
Write “what great looks like” for this level in one paragraph. Example: “L7 Director sets strategy for a 20–40 person org, hires bar-raising leaders, operates two levels down, and raises quality without heroics.”

Competency definitions and rubrics
For each must-have competency, define:
Behavioral definition: Clear description of the behavior you expect.
Probes: 2–3 structured questions to elicit repeatable evidence.
Signals: Positive/negative indicators to listen for.
Rubric: Ratings with anchored examples (Strong No/No/Leaning/Yes/Strong Yes).

Example competency
Competency: Ownership
Definition: Sees problems as theirs to solve across org boundaries; anticipates risks; doesn’t say “not my job.”
Probes: “Tell me about a time you took responsibility for a problem outside your remit. What was the outcome?” “Describe a missed goal. What did you do after you realized you’d miss?” “How have you prevented a recurrence of a past incident?”
Positive signals: Frames end-to-end problem; takes initiative without permission; names concrete preventative mechanisms; learns and applies.
Negative signals: Blames others; waits for direction; focuses on excuses over fixes.
Rubric anchors:
Strong Yes: Drove cross-org solution with durable mechanisms; measurable impact; repeat pattern across roles.
Yes: Took end-to-end responsibility within team; clear learning and prevention.
Leaning: Partial ownership; needed prompting; limited mechanism.
No: Deflection; narrow scope; no prevention.
Strong No: Ethical shortcuts; repeats failures; toxic blame.

Loop design template
Loop goals
Minimize variance, maximize signal, finish within 7–10 calendar days.

Loop roles and coverage
Assign one owner per competency. No overlaps, no gaps.
Hiring manager: Outcomes fit, Deliver Results, bar judgment.
Bar raiser or equivalent: Overall calibration, bias checks, tie-break, raise-the-bar assessment.
Peer(s): Technical/functional depth and collaboration.
Cross-functional partner: Influence and customer mindset.
Leadership interviewer: Team building, hiring/developing talent, culture add.

Interview plan
Screen: 30–45 minutes. Confirm must-have basics tied to outcomes.
Take-home or in-loop exercise (if applicable): Realistic, time-boxed, scored against rubric.
Onsite sequence: 4–6 interviews, 45–60 minutes each, with defined competencies and probes.
Breaks and buffer: Protect candidate energy; maintain consistency.

Candidate exercise guidance
Use tasks that mirror day one work. Provide inputs, constraints, and definition of done. Score on approach, tradeoffs, communication, and outcomes. Avoid spec-work or unpaid deliverables that create IP issues.

Interviewer packets
Include role scorecard, competency assignment, probes, rubric, and candidate resume. Require written notes within 24 hours: evidence first, vote second, confidence level, and flags.

Decision meeting and bar
Hold a single debrief with all interviewers and the bar raiser within 48 hours. Start with the role outcomes, then each owner presents evidence and rating against rubric. The bar raiser drives to a decision: Hire, Hold for more data (one targeted follow-up only), or No Hire. “Maybe” is a process failure.

Quality bar checklist
Every interviewer used assigned probes tied to the rubric.
Notes capture verbatim evidence and observed behaviors.
Ratings are against the bar, not the candidate pool.
Bias checks are explicit: similarity, recency, halo/horns, pedigree worship.
Decision references outcomes and must-have competencies, not charm.

Sample loop map (dummy)
L6 Software Engineering Manager
Outcomes: Ship two roadmap features; reduce incidents by 30%; hire 3 bar-raising engineers; stabilize on-call to sustainable load.
Must-haves: Technical Depth (services), Deliver Results, Ownership, Hiring/Developing Talent, Cross-Functional Influence, Customer Obsession.

Loop
HM: Deliver Results, outcomes fit.
Senior Eng Peer: Technical Depth.
Staff Eng from partner team: Cross-Functional Influence.
Peer EM: Hiring/Developing Talent.
PM partner: Customer Obsession.
Bar Raiser: Ownership and raise-the-bar judgment; debrief lead.
Exercise: Incident postmortem and improvement plan, 45 minutes, provided logs and metrics; score on root-cause clarity, mechanisms, comms, and tradeoffs.

Red flags and failure modes
Undefined bar: Vague outcomes, laundry-list JDs, or shifting criteria mid-loop.
Freelance questions: Interviewers “go fishing,” producing noisy, unscorable anecdotes.
Opinion masquerading as evidence: “Seemed sharp” without behaviors.
Speed over quality: Rushing to fill seat; you’ll pay with months of cleanup.
Consensus worship: Strong dissent ignored; bar raiser must surface and resolve.

Mechanisms to keep the bar high
Pre-brief: 15 minutes to align on bar, competencies, and probes.
Post-interview SLA: Notes and rating in 24 hours, or your vote is excluded.
Calibration library: Keep 3–5 anonymized, exemplary notes per competency as training references for new interviewers.
Audit: Quarterly review of hires vs. scorecards; check signal validity and adjust probes.

Offer decision template
Summary against outcomes: Where candidate is strong/weak relative to 6–12 month outcomes.
Competency table: Must-haves with ratings and 1–2 sentence evidence.
Risk and mitigation: Onboarding plan to de-risk gaps; assign mentors; 30/60/90.
Bar judgment: Hire or No Hire with rationale anchored to the bar, not feelings.

Post-hire feedback loop
At 90 days, compare onboarding progress to scorecard expectations. At 6 months, compare results to the role outcomes you wrote. If your loop predicted poorly, update the scorecard, probes, or training. The loop should learn.
