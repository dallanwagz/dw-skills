# Prioritization and Tradeoff Framework

## Purpose
Prioritization is the discipline of making deliberate choices about what to do, what to defer, and what to eliminate when workload exceeds capacity. It ensures that effort is aligned with high-impact outcomes rather than reactive urgency. Mastering tradeoffs is essential for maintaining velocity and preventing burnout in complex organizational environments.

## Core Principles

### 1. Understand the "Why"
Before executing, understand how a task connects to company goals, revenue, or customer value. Aligning work with strategic objectives ensures that effort is spent on what truly matters.

### 2. Use Frameworks, Not Feelings
Employ objective frameworks to categorize work and remove emotional bias or the "trap of the easy."

### 3. Articulate Tradeoffs Explicitly
Be transparent about what will *not* happen when a new priority is accepted. Explicit communication prevents surprises and allows stakeholders to course-correct based on realistic capacity.

### 4. Focus on One Thing at a Time
Multi-tasking dilutes impact. Prioritize the most critical outcome and work on it exclusively until it is completed or genuinely blocked.

## Prioritization Matrices

### The Eisenhower Matrix (Urgency vs. Importance)
- **Urgent & Important**: Do first (crises, hard deadlines).
- **Not Urgent & Important**: Schedule deliberately (strategic work, relationship building).
- **Urgent & Not Important**: Delegate or decline (interruptions, some meetings).
- **Not Urgent & Not Important**: Eliminate or batch (time wasters, low-value work).

### The Evans Matrix (Importance vs. Ease)
- **Important & Easy**: Quick wins; do these first to build momentum.
- **Important & Hard**: The real work; do these second (career-changing impact).
- **Not Important & Easy**: The drift; avoid these when energy is low.
- **Not Important & Hard**: Don't do these; they result in wasted effort and suffering.

## The Daily Ritual: "Eat the Frog"
Identify the single most important, often hardest, task each morning. Complete it before engaging with email, Slack, or meetings. Tackling the "frog" when your energy is highest ensures that the day's most critical outcome is achieved regardless of subsequent interruptions.

## Handling Capacity Overload
- **Communicate Early**: Flag potential delays or dropped tasks before they happen.
- **Ask for Specific Help**: Request resource reallocation or delegation for specific projects.
- **Force the Tradeoff**: "I can do X, but Y will be delayed. Which is the higher priority for you?"

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] The "why" and business impact of each priority are understood.
- [ ] Input from managers and key stakeholders has been gathered.
- [ ] Top priorities and explicit tradeoffs have been communicated.
- [ ] Important/Hard work is scheduled for peak energy times.
- [ ] Low-value tasks have been explicitly deferred or eliminated.
- [ ] One major task is being focused on at a time.
- [ ] Impact is measurable in terms of business value or team outcomes.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Reactive Prioritization**: Letting the loudest stakeholder or the most recent email dictate the day.
- **Hiding Tradeoffs**: Pretending everything can be done, leading to missed deadlines and poor quality.
- **The "Easy Work" Trap**: Drift toward comfortable, low-impact tasks when tired.
- **Silent Drops**: Allowing tasks to fail without proactive communication.
- **Multitasking**: Spreading effort too thin across too many "priorities."
