# Cross-Functional Project Briefs

## Purpose
Cross-functional project briefs are essential for eliminating ambiguity in large-scale engineering and product projects. They serve as a single source of truth and a contract between all stakeholders, capturing agreements on what's being built, why, and who is responsible.

## Core Principles

### 1. One Brief, One Source of Truth
Avoid document fragmentation. This brief is the reference point for all Slack discussions, status updates, and decision-making. If reality changes, update the brief and notify stakeholders.

### 2. Specific Success Criteria
Define success with measurable data, not vague goals. Use clear metrics (e.g., "Reduce latency by 20%") rather than generalities (e.g., "Improve performance").

### 3. Visible Tradeoffs
Explicitly name constraints in time, scope, and quality. Transparent tradeoffs build trust with stakeholders and prevent late-stage surprises.

### 4. Clear Ownership
Assign decision authority to specific individuals, not just roles or teams. Everyone should know exactly who makes the final call on prioritization, architecture, and delivery.

### 5. Strategic Context
Connect the project to larger company OKRs and team missions. Explaining *why* the work matters increases team buy-in and stakeholder alignment.

## Project Brief Template

### Overview
A 30-second summary of what is being built and why.

### Business Context & Goals
- OKR alignment
- Problem statement
- Target audience/beneficiaries

### In Scope
Specific, actionable items that engineering can plan against.

### Out of Scope
Explicit list of what will *not* be delivered in this phase to prevent scope creep.

### Success Criteria
Measurable, verifiable outcomes (e.g., "Launch by June 15", "Zero critical security findings").

### Timeline & Key Milestones
- Design & API Sign-off
- Alpha/Beta builds
- Security/Audit reviews
- Production Launch

### Ownership & Roles
- **Project Lead**: Decision authority on features and tradeoffs.
- **Engineering Lead**: Responsible for architecture and delivery.
- **Design/Security/CS Leads**: Specific domain responsibilities.

### Dependencies & Risks
| Risk | Impact | Mitigation Plan |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| [Risk Name] | [High/Med/Low] | [Concrete Action] |

### Known Tradeoffs
Explicitly document what is being sacrificed (e.g., "Scope vs. Timeline", "UX Polish vs. Ship Date").

### Open Questions
List of unresolved decisions with assigned owners and deadlines.

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Overview is clear and concise.
- [ ] Connected to company OKRs.
- [ ] In-scope items are actionable.
- [ ] Out-of-scope items are explicit.
- [ ] Success criteria are measurable.
- [ ] Ownership is assigned to specific names.
- [ ] Risks have real mitigation plans.
- [ ] Tradeoffs are named and reasoned.
- [ ] Stakeholders have reviewed the draft.
- [ ] Stored in an accessible, central location.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Vague Success Criteria**: Ensure goals are data-driven.
- **Hiding Tradeoffs**: Be upfront about what isn't being built.
- **No Real Ownership**: Ensure decision authority is clear.
- **Excessive Length**: Keep it to 1-2 pages.
- **Stale Content**: Update the brief whenever project reality changes.
