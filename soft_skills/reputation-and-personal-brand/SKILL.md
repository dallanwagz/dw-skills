# Reputation and Personal Brand

## Purpose
Your reputation is how you are described when you are not in the room. Your personal brand is the intentional strategy you use to shape that reputation through your actions, communications, and visibility. Together, they form the foundation of your professional influence and determine the opportunities you are granted. A strong, authentic reputation is a multiplier for every other leadership skill, while a damaged or vague one acts as a persistent headwind.

## Core Principles

### 1. Perception Is the Starting Point
Reputation is built on how others experience you, not on your internal intentions. Acknowledging current perceptions—whether they feel "fair" or not—is the first step toward intentional change.

### 2. Built Through Consistency
Reputation is rarely forged in grand gestures. It is the cumulative result of hundreds of small, kept commitments: hitting deadlines, following through, and treating others with respect over time.

### 3. Track Record Is Credibility
A consistent history of delivery creates a "trust buffer." When things go wrong, a strong track record earns you the benefit of the doubt; a weak one defaults to skepticism.

### 4. Behavior Under Pressure
Your reputation is most accurately defined by how you act during crises, failures, and high-stakes disagreements. Professionalism in easy times is expected; integrity under pressure is what people remember.

### 5. Authenticity Is Required
A personal brand must be grounded in who you actually are and what you genuinely value. Attempting to perform a "character" is unsustainable and easily detected by others.

## The Three Layers of Personal Brand

### Layer 1: Organizational Reputation
The pattern of how you are perceived within your current company. Built through reliable delivery, fair treatment of colleagues, and transparent decision-making.

### Layer 2: Industry or Field Reputation
How you are known by peers outside your organization. Built through public learning, sharing expertise (writing, speaking), and contributing to professional communities.

### Layer 3: Your Personal Narrative
The story you tell about your own work and career. A strong narrative is specific, results-oriented, and connects your past accomplishments to your future goals.

## Strategic Reputation Building

### Step 1: Define Your Target Reputation
Identify one or two specific qualities you want to be known for (e.g., "The person who delivers on hard deadlines" or "A world-class developer of talent").

### Step 2: Identify Supporting Behaviors
Determine the specific, observable actions that would lead someone to describe you in those terms. If you want to be known as a bridge-builder, you must consistently seek cross-team alignment.

### Step 3: Execute Consistently
Reputation requires repetition. You must perform these supporting behaviors regularly, especially when it is inconvenient or difficult.

### Step 4: Make Impact Visible
Visibility is not about bragging; it is about ensuring that your results and thinking are present and understood. Use data-driven updates, credit your collaborators, and share your learnings broadly.

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] I have sought honest feedback to understand my current reputation.
- [ ] I have identified the specific "patterns" in how others perceive me.
- [ ] I am clear on the one or two core things I want to be known for.
- [ ] My daily behaviors explicitly support the reputation I am trying to build.
- [ ] I am communicating my results using specific, data-driven examples.
- [ ] I am consistently crediting others for their contributions.
- [ ] I am taking full ownership of failures and presenting clear remediation plans.
- [ ] I am investing in Layer 2 visibility (industry/field) beyond my internal role.
- [ ] My personal brand story is authentic and grounded in real accomplishments.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Confusing Title with Reputation**: Assuming a promotion automatically increases your organizational influence.
- **Over-Promising, Under-Delivering**: Destroying credibility by failing to follow through on commitments.
- **Blaming Others for Your Reputation**: Failing to take ownership of how you are perceived by stakeholders.
- **Inconsistent Rituals**: Behaving professionally only when it is easy or convenient.
- **Inauthentic Branding**: Attempting to project a professional image that is disconnected from your actual skills or values.
