# Performance Feedback and Difficult Conversations

## Purpose
Performance feedback and difficult conversations are essential tools for leadership, accountability, and professional development. Effective feedback builds trust and accelerates growth, while poorly handled conversations can damage relationships and organizational culture. This skill focuses on delivering hard truths with compassion, clarity, and a focus on observable behavior rather than personal identity.

## Core Principles

### 1. Feedback Impacts Identity
Recognize that negative feedback can trigger defensive emotional responses (fear, shame). Navigate these emotions by framing feedback as coaching and growth, not punishment.

### 2. Perception Is Reality for Career Growth
Address gaps in perception even if they don't align with the individual's intent. Being perceived as having a performance gap is a real obstacle that must be managed.

### 3. Praise Publicly, Criticize Privately
Always deliver critical feedback in private to respect the individual's ego and provide a safe space for honest dialogue.

### 4. Preparation Is Key
The success of a difficult conversation is determined before it starts. Leaders must manage their own emotions and gain clarity on facts and desired outcomes before meeting.

## Five Rules for Hard Conversations

### 1. Manage Your Emotions First
Ensure you are calm and objective before the conversation. Humans subconsciously read and react to a leader's emotional state.

### 2. Choose the Right Time and Place
Avoid ambushing or cornering individuals. Schedule a dedicated time and provide the high-level topic in advance to reduce anxiety.

### 3. Set the Stage
Start by reaffirming your commitment to the person's success. Frame the conversation as a collaborative effort to remove obstacles to their growth.

### 4. Be Open to Being Wrong
Approach the conversation with curiosity. You may be missing critical context or information that changes the nature of the feedback.

### 5. Listen First, Decide Second
Ask open-ended questions to understand their perspective. Truly hear their side before finalizing your assessment or next steps.

## The SBI Model: Feedback That Lands
Use the Situation-Behavior-Impact model to keep feedback factual and depersonalized:

- **Situation**: Define the specific time and place (e.g., "During yesterday's architecture review...").
- **Behavior**: Describe the observable action without judgment (e.g., "You dismissed three proposals without explaining why...").
- **Impact**: Explain the consequences of that behavior (e.g., "The team has become hesitant to share new ideas, slowing down our innovation...").

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Emotions are managed; I am calm and clear-headed.
- [ ] Meeting is scheduled at a respectful time with a clear topic.
- [ ] Prepared with specific SBI examples.
- [ ] Posture is one of curiosity and partnership.
- [ ] Clear on the specific behavior change required.
- [ ] Ready to handle potential defensiveness or pushback.
- [ ] Plan in place for follow-up and accountability.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Waiting Too Long**: Deliver feedback when it is timely and relevant.
- **Burying the Lead**: Don't soften the message so much that the core feedback is lost.
- **Attacking Character**: Focus on changeable behaviors, not immutable personality traits.
- **Failing to Follow Up**: Accountability requires checking back to see if behaviors have changed.
- **One-Way Monologue**: Feedback should be a dialogue where both parties listen and adjust.
