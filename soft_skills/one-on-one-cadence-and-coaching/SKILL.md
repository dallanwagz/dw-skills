# One-on-One Cadence and Coaching

## Purpose
The one-on-one is the most critical recurring meeting for a leader. Its primary purpose is to maintain alignment, build trust, and provide a dedicated space for coaching and development. Unlike status meetings, one-on-ones focus on the individual—their blockers, their growth edges, and their strategic thinking. Consistent, high-quality one-on-ones prevent crises and ensure that feedback is continuous rather than seasonal.

## Core Principles

### 1. The Foundation of Leadership
One-on-ones are the primary mechanism for delegation, feedback, and talent development. They are the space where organizational culture is reinforced one conversation at a time.

### 2. Trust and Alignment First
Effective coaching is only possible if there is mutual trust and clear expectations. Dedicate early sessions to establishing these foundational elements.

### 3. The Report Owns the Agenda
While the manager can add items, the direct report should drive the starting point of the conversation. What they choose to discuss provides valuable data on their priorities and concerns.

### 4. Align Individual and Organizational Goals
Coaching should find the intersection between what the company needs and what the individual wants to achieve. Investing in their growth drives long-term commitment.

### 5. Predictability Is Sacred
Schedule one-on-ones at the same day and time every week. Rescheduling should be rare; consistency signals that the individual is a priority.

### 6. Coaching Is Asking, Not Telling
Avoid the trap of immediately providing solutions based on your experience. Real growth happens when reports are guided to their own insights through thoughtful questioning.

## One-on-One Architecture (30-60 Minutes)

### 1. Human Check-in (5 min)
A brief, genuine assessment of the individual's current state (energy, stress, motivation). This emotional baseline informs the coaching approach.

### 2. Their Priorities & Blockers (10-15 min)
Review what they are working on, what is going well, and where they are stuck. Listen for patterns in how they handle obstacles.

### 3. Feedback & Coaching (10-15 min)
Provide timely, specific feedback using the SBI (Situation-Behavior-Impact) model. Use this time for "in-the-moment" coaching around current tasks.

### 4. Strategic Alignment (5-10 min)
Share context on broader company/team priorities and clarify how their work contributes to these goals.

### 5. Growth & Development (5-10 min)
Check in on their long-term growth plan and specific development goals.

### 6. Summary & Commitments (5 min)
Summarize key decisions, assigned actions, and follow-up items for both parties.

## Coaching Question Framework
Use questions to guide reports toward agency and self-awareness:
- **Clarifying**: "Help me understand what you mean by..."
- **Reflective**: "If you were in their shoes, what would your read be?"
- **Forward-looking**: "What would it look like if this were working perfectly?"
- **Accountability**: "What is your next specific step to resolve this?"
- **Developmental**: "What is the primary lesson you're taking from this challenge?"

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Meetings are scheduled at a consistent, non-negotiable time each week.
- [ ] The report provides the initial agenda items.
- [ ] Manager listens more than they talk (curiosity over authority).
- [ ] No laptops or phones; focus is 100% on the individual.
- [ ] Feedback is delivered using specific SBI examples.
- [ ] Follow-ups from the previous week are addressed.
- [ ] Questions are used to stimulate thinking rather than giving direct advice.
- [ ] Feedback on the manager's performance is requested regularly.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Status Meeting Drift**: Spending the entire time on project updates that could be handled async.
- **Frequent Rescheduling**: Erasing the individual's sense of priority and damaging trust.
- **Lack of Follow-through**: Failing to remember or address commitments made in previous sessions.
- **Providing Immediate Solutions**: Robbing the report of the opportunity to develop their own problem-solving skills.
- **Ignoring the Human Element**: Failing to recognize when stress or personal factors are impacting performance.
