# Project Kickoff and Definition of Done

## Purpose
A project kickoff is the pivotal transition from planning to execution, ensuring all team members and stakeholders are aligned on goals, roles, and expectations. The "Definition of Done" (DoD) is a shared contract that explicitly defines what "finished" means, preventing quality gaps and late-stage surprises. Together, they provide the foundation for clear communication and accountability throughout the project lifecycle.

## Core Principles

### 1. Kickoff Is Confirmation, Not Discovery
Before the kickoff, all major planning, stakeholder mapping, and risk assessments must be complete. The meeting serves to confirm the blueprint and ensure everyone is ready to execute.

### 2. DoD Prevents Ambiguity
Vague completion criteria lead to misaligned expectations. A specific, measurable DoD ensures that everyone—from engineering to product and sales—agrees on the quality bar before a project is handed off.

### 3. Establish Escalation Norms
A successful kickoff creates psychological safety by explicitly defining how to handle blockers, who makes specific decisions, and when to escalate issues.

### 4. Define What Won't Happen
Clarify constraints and out-of-scope items during kickoff to prevent scope creep and manage stakeholder expectations regarding polish and documentation.

## Project Kickoff Agenda (60-90 Minutes)

### 1. The Mission
Clearly state the "why" behind the project. Connect the work to company strategy and customer value (e.g., "Unlocking Q3 revenue by addressing top enterprise security requests").

### 2. Scope & Deliverables
Review what is being built and what is intentionally excluded. Use mockups or diagrams to ensure visual alignment.

### 3. Success Criteria
Define measurable outcomes (e.g., "Launch by June 15", "Zero critical security findings", "60% adoption within 30 days").

### 4. Ownership & Authority
Assign decision-making authority to specific individuals for prioritization, architecture, and stakeholder management.

### 5. Risks & Mitigations
Identify top risks and present concrete mitigation plans. Name constraints explicitly.

### 6. Communication & Escalation
Set the cadence for syncs and status updates. Define the "24-hour rule" for blockers and the specific path for escalation.

### 7. Timeline & Milestones
Walk through the critical path and key dates for design, alpha/beta builds, audits, and final launch.

## Definition of Done (DoD) Template
A project is "Done" only when:
- **Code & Quality**: All code is reviewed, tested (unit, integration, E2E), and meets performance benchmarks.
- **Security & Compliance**: Security audits are complete, and zero critical vulnerabilities remain.
- **Documentation**: API docs, internal runbooks, and customer-facing support materials are updated.
- **Stakeholder Approval**: Key owners have signed off on the final implementation.
- **Observability**: Metrics, logging, and alerting are in place to monitor post-launch health.
- **Deployment**: Code is successfully merged and deployed to the target environment.

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Pre-kickoff document sent to team one week in advance.
- [ ] The "mission" is clearly linked to business goals.
- [ ] Ownership and decision authority are assigned to specific names.
- [ ] Escalation path is understood by all team members.
- [ ] Success criteria are specific and data-driven.
- [ ] Definition of Done is documented and shared.
- [ ] Out-of-scope items are explicitly named.
- [ ] Timeline includes realistic buffers for audits and feedback.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Under-preparing**: Entering kickoff without a solid plan.
- **Vague Metrics**: Using subjective measures like "customer satisfaction" without a way to track it.
- **Ignoring Authority**: Failing to empower individuals to make decisions without leadership intervention.
- **Stale DoD**: Not updating the Definition of Done when project realities or constraints shift mid-stream.
- **Silent Blockers**: Not reinforcing the escalation path, leading to hidden risks.
